Periodic Trends

Group 1: Melting Points (Metallic Bonding)

Down Group 1, atomic radius increases. The distance between the positive nucleus and the sea of delocalized electrons becomes greater. This weakens the electrostatic attraction (metallic bond), causing the melting point to decrease.

Select Element

Lithium

180.5°C

Smallest atomic radius. Strongest electrostatic attraction between nucleus and delocalized electrons.

Group 1: Reactivity (Losing an Electron)

Group 1 metals react by losing their single outer valence electron. Going down the group, the outer electron is further away and heavily shielded by inner shells. This makes it easier to lose, meaning elements at the bottom are more reactive.

Electron Lost!
Select Element

Lithium

Valence electron is close to the nucleus. High electrostatic pull makes it less reactive.

Group 7: Melting & Boiling Points (Intermolecular Forces)

Group 7 elements exist as diatomic molecules (e.g., F₂, Cl₂). Going down the group, molecules become larger with more electrons. This increases the strength of the intermolecular forces (van der Waals), requiring more energy to separate them, increasing the melting/boiling point.

Select Element

Fluorine

State: Gas (BP: -188°C)

Smallest molecules. Very weak intermolecular forces, so it is a gas at room temperature.

Group 7: Reactivity (Gaining an Electron)

Group 7 elements react by gaining one electron to complete their outer shell. Going down the group, the outer shell is further away and more shielded. The nucleus has a harder time attracting a foreign electron, making the element less reactive.

Electron Gained!
Select Element

Fluorine

Small atomic radius with little shielding. Can violently attract an electron to complete its octet. Highly reactive!
